# Reframing Techniques

*"We're looking at this from one angle — change the lens."*

These techniques change how you see the idea without changing the idea
itself. Different viewpoint, different language, different form,
different emotional register. The idea stays the same; the frame
shifts. Reframing is the fastest way to unstick a riff that's
circling — same territory looks completely different when you walk
around to the other side.

### Perspective shift

View the idea through someone else's eyes. A first-time user, a
skeptic, a competitor, someone in a different industry, a different
culture, a different generation. This also includes designing FOR a
different audience — not just imagining their reaction, but rebuilding
the idea's priorities around what they'd actually need.

Perspective shift works because most ideas get designed from one
vantage point and stay there. The founder's perspective, the power
user's perspective, the Silicon Valley perspective. Every new vantage
point surfaces assumptions that were invisible from the original
angle. In a riff, this is the fastest way to break out of a groove
where everyone is nodding along.

**Good examples:**

- **Riffing on a code review tool.** Conversation has been focused on
  senior dev workflows. Shift: "What does this look like to someone
  on their first week at a new job? They don't know the codebase,
  don't know the norms, and every review comment feels like
  judgment." Suddenly the tool becomes about psychological safety and
  learning, not just catching bugs. New threads: onboarding mode,
  anonymous review option, "common pattern in this codebase" tooltips.

- **Riffing on a local events app.** Everyone's assumed the user is
  20-something and social. Shift: "How does a recently retired person
  in a small town use this? They have time, they want connection, but
  they don't want to feel like they're on a dating app." New threads:
  skill-sharing, recurring low-pressure meetups, "I'll be at the
  coffee shop" passive signaling.

- **Riffing on a developer portfolio platform.** Shift to a hiring
  manager in a non-tech company: "I don't know what half these
  technologies mean. I need to hire someone who can build our
  internal tool, and I'm looking at portfolios full of jargon." New
  threads: plain-language project summaries, outcome-focused
  showcases, "explain this to a non-technical stakeholder" mode.

- **Riffing on a CLI productivity tool.** Shift to a competitor:
  "If I'm building the rival product, what's the obvious weakness I'd
  attack? What's the feature gap I'd build my marketing around?" New
  threads: the things you've been avoiding because they're hard, the
  audience segment you've been ignoring, the adjacent tool you should
  have integrated with.

**Anti-examples:**

- Don't: "What would a user in Japan think?" and then just add
  language translation. That's surface-level — a real perspective
  shift would explore different cultural relationships to the
  problem space itself (different privacy expectations, different
  social dynamics, different technology habits).

- Don't: "What would a CEO think?" → "They'd want a dashboard."
  This is a cliche, not a perspective shift. Push past the stock
  answer to what a specific kind of leader actually struggles with.

- Don't: Shifting perspective and then immediately solving for that
  perspective. The point is to generate new angles, not to redesign
  the product for every possible user. Stay in riff mode.

**When to deploy:** Conversation has settled into one viewpoint for
3+ exchanges. Everyone agrees. The same kind of user keeps being
referenced. The idea feels "obvious" — perspective shift makes it
unfamiliar again.

**See also:** Misuse exploration (below) for an extreme perspective
shift. Narrative seeding (below) for embedding a perspective in a
story.

### Word pivot

Pick a key word in the current framing and swap it. Synonym,
metaphor, adjacent concept — each substitution reframes the entire
idea. "Network" becomes "ecosystem" becomes "organism" becomes
"nervous system" becomes "market." The idea hasn't changed, but each
word pulls different associations, different structures, different
possibilities into focus.

Word pivot works because language constrains thought more than we
realize. The word you chose to describe the idea is already filtering
what you can imagine. Swapping that word doesn't change the idea — it
changes what ideas can attach to it. In a riff, this is the micro
move: quick, low-effort, surprisingly generative.

**Good examples:**

- **Riffing on a team "dashboard."** Pivot the word "dashboard."
  Dashboard → cockpit → control room → watchtower → lighthouse.
  "Cockpit" suggests individual piloting with instruments.
  "Watchtower" suggests passive surveillance of a landscape.
  "Lighthouse" suggests broadcasting a signal that helps others
  navigate. Each word reshapes what the thing does: active control
  vs. broad awareness vs. helping others orient.

- **Riffing on a "notification" system.** Pivot "notification."
  Notification → nudge → whisper → tap on the shoulder → rumor.
  "Nudge" is behavioral design. "Whisper" is intimate and private.
  "Tap on the shoulder" is human and interruptive. "Rumor" is
  ambient and social — information that spreads without being
  pushed. Each reframe produces a different system.

- **Riffing on a knowledge "base."** Pivot "base." Base → garden →
  compost heap → river → toolbox. "Garden" needs tending and grows
  over time. "Compost heap" takes discarded scraps and turns them
  into something fertile. "River" flows and you step in at different
  points. Suddenly a static wiki has five different possible
  personalities.

**Anti-examples:**

- Don't: Pivot to a word and then not explore it. "Dashboard →
  cockpit. Anyway, back to the dashboard..." The value is in sitting
  with the new word long enough to see what it reveals.

- Don't: Pick words that are too close. "Platform → system →
  solution" — these are corporate synonyms, not real pivots. The
  swap needs to pull from a different conceptual space to produce
  new associations.

**When to deploy:** The framing feels locked in. The same word keeps
appearing. People are building on the word's assumptions without
questioning them. A quick pivot can break the frame in thirty seconds.

**See also:** Metaphor mutation (below) for evolving a framing
through successive steps rather than a single swap.

### Metaphor mutation

Take a metaphor that's already working in the conversation and evolve
it through successive steps. Each mutation pushes the metaphor
further, and each step generates ideas the previous step couldn't
reach. "Marketplace" becomes "auction" becomes "game show" becomes
"talent competition." The metaphor is a vehicle — mutation drives it
into new territory.

This differs from word pivot in scope: word pivot swaps a single
term, metaphor mutation takes a whole conceptual frame and walks it
through transformations. It works because metaphors are productive —
they generate ideas just by existing — but they also have limits. A
marketplace metaphor generates marketplace ideas. Mutating it past
its comfort zone is where the interesting stuff lives.

**Good examples:**

- **Riffing on a mentorship platform described as "matchmaking."**
  Mutate the metaphor: matchmaking → apprenticeship → relay race →
  hive mind. "Matchmaking" implies pairing two people.
  "Apprenticeship" implies long-term investment and skill transfer.
  "Relay race" implies sequential handoffs — maybe mentorship isn't
  one person, it's a chain of people each contributing a leg. "Hive
  mind" implies collective intelligence — maybe the mentor is a
  crowd, not an individual.

- **Riffing on a budgeting app described as a "diet for spending."**
  Mutate: diet → training regimen → game season → gardening season.
  "Diet" implies restriction. "Training regimen" implies building
  toward a goal with progressive overload. "Game season" implies
  intense bursts with an offseason. "Gardening season" implies
  planting now for harvest later. Each mutation reframes the user's
  relationship with their money.

- **Riffing on a project management tool called a "kanban board."**
  Mutate "board": board → map → terrain → weather system. "Map"
  implies navigation and exploration. "Terrain" implies obstacles
  and elevation — some work is uphill. "Weather system" implies
  forces acting on you that you can predict but not control. A
  project management tool that shows you the forecast, not just the
  plan.

**Anti-examples:**

- Don't: Mutate without pausing to harvest. Each step should produce
  at least one idea before you move to the next. Rattling through
  "marketplace → auction → game show → talent show → reality TV"
  without stopping is just free association — the technique is
  generative mutation, not a word chain.

- Don't: Force mutations that don't connect. If "marketplace →
  auction" is productive but "auction → volcano" doesn't spark
  anything, don't push it. Mutation steps should feel like they
  twist the lens, not shatter it.

**When to deploy:** An analogy or metaphor is already in play and
producing ideas, but the thread is starting to repeat. The metaphor
has given what it can at its current form. Mutate it forward instead
of abandoning it.

**See also:** Word pivot (above) for single-word reframes. Analogy
(techniques-connection.md) for introducing a new domain parallel.

### Medium shift

Change the form of the idea entirely. If it's an app, what if it
were a physical object? A game? A newsletter? A protocol? A public
installation? A story? Forcing the idea into a different medium
strips away the assumptions of the current form and reveals the
essence — what the idea actually IS versus how it's currently
expressed.

Medium shift works because form and function get tangled together.
When you've been riffing on "an app that does X," every idea is
shaped by app conventions — screens, taps, notifications, user
accounts. Shift the medium and those conventions evaporate. What
survives the shift is the core. What doesn't survive was just a
convention you'd mistaken for the idea.

**Good examples:**

- **Riffing on a habit tracking app.** Medium shift: "What if this
  were a physical object on your desk?" A cube you rotate to
  different faces for different habits. A jar you add a marble to.
  A plant that grows. Strip the screens away and the core reveals
  itself: it's about making invisible progress visible and tactile.
  New threads: ambient displays, physical-digital hybrid, widget
  that acts like a desk object.

- **Riffing on a developer learning platform.** Medium shift: "What
  if this were a game?" Not gamification (badges, points) — actually
  a game. Puzzle levels where the puzzle IS the API you're learning.
  Boss fights that are real debugging challenges. A world map that
  IS the technology landscape. Progression that mirrors actual skill
  development. New threads: procedurally generated challenges from
  real codebases, multiplayer debugging.

- **Riffing on an internal company wiki.** Medium shift: "What if
  this were a physical space?" A building with rooms for each team.
  Hallways where you overhear adjacent conversations. A lobby where
  new things are displayed. A basement archive. Suddenly information
  architecture has spatial intuition: proximity, discovery through
  wandering, the feeling of "oh, I didn't know this room existed."

- **Riffing on a meeting scheduling tool.** Medium shift: "What if
  this were a protocol instead of a product?" Not an app, but a
  shared convention — like how email works. A standard format for
  expressing availability. A way for any tool to interoperate. Now
  the idea is about infrastructure, not interfaces.

**Anti-examples:**

- Don't: "What if it were a game?" → "Add points and
  leaderboards." That's gamification bolted onto the existing form,
  not a medium shift. Actually reimagine it AS a game with game
  mechanics, game structure, game logic.

- Don't: Shift the medium and then immediately shift back. "What if
  it were a physical object? Interesting. Anyway, back to the app."
  The point is to stay in the shifted medium long enough to find
  something you'd never have found in the original form.

- Don't: Pick a medium that's too similar. "What if the mobile app
  were a desktop app?" That's a platform change, not a medium shift.
  The shift needs to be big enough to break assumptions.

**When to deploy:** The idea is trapped in its current form. Every
new thought is shaped by app conventions (or web conventions, or API
conventions). The riff needs to escape the container to find the
essence.

### Emotion-driven ideation

Start from a feeling instead of a function. Not "what should it do?"
but "what should it feel like?" What would a delightful version look
like? A calming one? An empowering one? A mischievous one? An
intimate one? Each emotional target produces different features,
different interactions, different priorities. The idea is the same;
the emotional register shifts.

This works because most tech ideation is function-first: what does
it do, what problem does it solve, what's the use case. Emotion-first
flips the hierarchy. A "calming" todo app is fundamentally different
from an "empowering" one — same problem, same domain, completely
different product. In a riff, this breaks the pattern when everything
has become a feature list.

**Good examples:**

- **Riffing on a team standup tool.** Everything's been about
  efficiency — async updates, time tracking, blockers. Emotion
  shift: "What would a standup that felt like a campfire feel like?"
  Warm, intimate, low-pressure. People sharing stories, not status
  updates. Unstructured check-ins. A space where "I'm having a rough
  week" is a valid standup. New threads: mood check-ins, storytelling
  prompts, audio-first standups that feel like a phone call with
  friends.

- **Riffing on a personal finance app.** Conversation stuck on
  budgets and categories. Emotion shift: "What would an empowering
  version feel like?" One that makes you feel capable, not guilty.
  That celebrates what you DID afford, not what you overspent on.
  That frames saving as strength, not deprivation. New threads:
  "financial wins" feed, strength-based messaging, progress
  framing that mirrors fitness apps.

- **Riffing on a documentation tool.** Everything's about structure
  and search. Emotion shift: "What would a playful version look
  like?" Docs that have personality. Easter eggs in the API
  reference. A tone that makes reading docs feel like reading a
  good blog post, not a legal contract. New threads: voice and tone
  as a feature, community-contributed examples with personality,
  "fun mode" that explains concepts through absurd analogies.

- **Riffing on a CI/CD pipeline tool.** Emotion shift: "What would
  a version that felt like a pit crew feel like?" Fast, precise,
  coordinated, adrenaline-fueled. Every second counts. The
  interface communicates urgency and precision. Builds feel like
  race laps — you're always trying to beat your time. New threads:
  real-time build racing, deployment speedrun metrics, team
  coordination as choreography.

**Anti-examples:**

- Don't: "What would a delightful version look like?" → "Add
  animations and confetti." Delight isn't decoration. It's a
  fundamental orientation toward surprising, pleasing, and
  respecting the user. Dig deeper than surface polish.

- Don't: Use emotion-driven ideation and then immediately evaluate
  whether the emotional version is "practical." The point is to
  generate from the emotion first, judge later. Killing the vibe
  mid-technique defeats the purpose.

**When to deploy:** Ideation has become a feature list. Everything is
functional and rational. Nobody has talked about how anything feels.
The riff needs warmth, texture, personality — not more capabilities.

### Narrative seeding

Turn the idea into a story. "A user wakes up and..." or "It's
Tuesday afternoon and..." — put a person in a moment with a need, and
let the story unfold. Ideas fall out naturally because stories demand
specificity. You can't tell a story about "a user" — you need a
person, a context, a motivation, a sequence of events. That
specificity surfaces ideas that abstract thinking misses.

Narrative seeding works because stories are the natural habitat of
human understanding. When ideation gets abstract — talking about
"systems" and "flows" and "architectures" — grounding it in one
person's Tuesday afternoon makes everything concrete. The story
doesn't need to be long. Even a few sentences of "someone does X,
then Y happens" reveals gaps and opportunities.

**Good examples:**

- **Riffing on a neighborhood safety app.** Abstract discussion about
  "community reporting" and "incident tracking" isn't landing. Seed:
  "It's 11pm. Maria hears glass breaking outside. She's home alone.
  She doesn't want to call 911 — it's probably nothing — but she
  wants to know if anyone else heard it." Now the ideas are
  specific: ambient awareness without alarmism, low-stakes
  check-ins, "did anyone else notice?" as a feature, passive
  reassurance.

- **Riffing on a developer onboarding tool.** Seed: "It's Kai's
  first day. They've cloned the repo. There are 200 files and no
  obvious entry point. Their mentor is in a different timezone and
  won't be online for 4 hours. They want to look competent by the
  first standup tomorrow." Now the riff is concrete: guided repo
  tours, "start here" markers left by previous newcomers,
  time-zone-aware mentorship, "what I wish I'd known" annotations
  from recent hires.

- **Riffing on an API marketplace.** Seed: "It's Friday. Priya is
  building a hackathon project. She needs a geocoding API, a
  sentiment analysis API, and something for sending SMS. She has 6
  hours and zero budget." Now the riff is about: free tier discovery,
  "hackathon bundles," instant integration templates, the difference
  between browsing and urgently shopping.

**Anti-examples:**

- Don't: Write a full short story. The narrative seed is a setup — 2
  to 4 sentences that create a specific person in a specific moment.
  Then riff on what that scenario reveals. Don't get lost in
  storytelling for its own sake.

- Don't: "A user opens the app and uses the main feature." That's
  not a story, it's a wireframe walkthrough. Narrative seeding needs
  a real situation with real stakes and real context, not a feature
  demo.

- Don't: Seed a narrative and then abandon it immediately. "Maria
  hears glass breaking. Anyway, we could add a reporting feature."
  Stay in the story. Let Maria's experience drive the ideas.

**When to deploy:** Abstract discussion isn't producing concrete
ideas. The conversation is about "users" and "systems" instead of
people and moments. Drop a specific scenario and let the story
generate what abstraction can't.

### Misuse exploration

Ask: "What's the weirdest way someone could use this? What's the
unintended use case? What would a troll do? What would a teenager
do? What would someone use this for that you'd never put in the
marketing?" Misuse scenarios reveal assumptions about intended use
and surface legitimate use cases hiding in the margins. The best
features often come from the "wrong" way to use something.

Misuse exploration works because intended use is a tiny fraction of
actual use. Twitter was for status updates, then it became a news
platform. Slack was for work, then it became community infrastructure.
Spreadsheets were for accounting, then they became everything. The
gap between intended and actual use is where the most interesting
ideas live. In a riff, deliberately exploring that gap produces
angles that "proper" ideation would never reach.

**Good examples:**

- **Riffing on a team retrospective tool.** "What if someone used
  this for something that isn't a retro?" Book club discussions.
  Family vacation planning. Band practice postmortems. D&D session
  debriefs. Each "misuse" reveals what the tool actually provides —
  structured group reflection — separated from its assumed context
  of software teams. New threads: domain-agnostic reflection tool,
  "retro as a format" for any group activity, templates for
  non-work use cases that make the tool sticky in people's personal
  lives.

- **Riffing on a focus timer app.** "What's the weirdest use?" Using
  it as a turn timer for board games. As a constraint for speed
  cooking. As a meditation timer. As a way to limit doomscrolling
  by literally making your phone unusable for 25 minutes. Each
  misuse surfaces a different core value: fairness (equal turns),
  creativity-under-constraint, mindfulness, self-control. New
  threads: "focus mode" as a platform others can build on, social
  timers, competitive focus sessions.

- **Riffing on a code snippet sharing tool.** "What would a troll
  do?" Share intentionally buggy code to waste people's time. Share
  code that looks helpful but has subtle security flaws. Create
  snippet chains that are actually a treasure hunt. The troll
  scenarios reveal: trust and verification matter, provenance
  matters, playful use is possible. New threads: verified snippets,
  community trust scores, intentionally using the "treasure hunt"
  idea for learning — guided discovery through linked snippets.

- **Riffing on a workplace recognition tool.** "What would someone
  use this for that you'd never put on the website?" Passive-
  aggressive "recognition" ("Great job finishing that report that
  was due last week!"). Flirting. Inside jokes. Political
  maneuvering. Each misuse reveals a social dynamic the tool would
  need to navigate. New threads: tone detection, private vs. public
  recognition, the difference between recognition and communication.

**Anti-examples:**

- Don't: "What if someone used it for illegal activity?" That's a
  compliance conversation, not a riff. Misuse exploration is about
  unexpected legitimate uses and what they reveal, not about abuse
  scenarios that lead to terms-of-service discussions.

- Don't: List misuses without exploring them. "Someone could use it
  for book clubs, or cooking, or gaming." OK, and? The value is in
  what each misuse reveals about the idea's actual essence. Stay
  with each one long enough to harvest the insight.

**When to deploy:** The intended use case feels too narrow or too
obvious. The conversation is only exploring the "proper" use. The
idea's boundaries are assumed but unexamined. Misuse exploration
widens the aperture by asking what happens at the edges.

**See also:** Perspective shift (above) for structured viewpoint
changes. Inversion (techniques-mutation.md) for flipping assumptions
rather than exploring edges.
