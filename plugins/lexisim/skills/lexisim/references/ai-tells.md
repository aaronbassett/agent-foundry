# AI Writing Tells

A catalog of patterns, phrases, and structural habits that signal AI-generated text. Use this reference during Phase 6 (Anti-Voice Definition) and when building the rejection list for the voice profile. During the anti-voice phase, users select archetypes their voice is explicitly NOT, and all associated patterns get added to the rejection list.

---

## Common AI Phrases

### Hedging & Qualifying

These phrases appear when the model is uncertain or padding for length:

- "It's worth noting that..."
- "It bears mentioning..."
- "It should be noted that..."
- "What's interesting is..."
- "Interestingly enough..."
- "Perhaps more importantly..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "Needless to say..."

### Generic Openers

Vague contextual framing that adds no meaning:

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In an era of..."
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."
- "As we navigate the complexities of..."
- "In the realm of..."
- "When it comes to..."

### Overblown Attribution

Making mundane things sound profound:

- "This serves as a testament to..."
- "This speaks volumes about..."
- "This underscores the importance of..."
- "This highlights the need for..."
- "This is a game-changer for..."

### Forced Engagement

Artificial intimacy with the reader:

- "Let's dive in..."
- "Let's unpack this..."
- "Let's explore..."
- "Buckle up..."
- "Spoiler alert:..."
- "Here's the thing..."
- "Here's the kicker..."

### Setup Labels

Declarative topic labels that announce what's coming instead of just stating it. These differ from forced engagement (which are collaborative invitations like "Let's dive in") because setup labels are mid-text announcements that telegraph the point before making it:

- "The key insight:"
- "The key takeaway:"
- "The important thing:"
- "Here's what matters:"
- "What you need to know:"
- "The reality is:"
- "The truth is:"
- "The real issue:"
- "The core problem:"
- "The critical point:"
- "One key thing:"
- "One thing to note:"
- "Simply put:"
- "In short:"

**Why it's a tell**: Human writers make the point directly. AI labels the point before making it, creating a redundant announcement layer. "The key insight is that teams ship faster with fewer meetings" vs "Teams ship faster with fewer meetings."

### Empty Emphasis

Words that sound strong but add nothing:

- "Truly"
- "Remarkably"
- "Incredibly" (as filler, not for actual disbelief)
- "Absolutely"
- "Undeniably"
- "Pivotal"
- "Crucial"
- "Groundbreaking"
- "Revolutionary"
- "Transformative"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "Robust" (especially for non-engineering contexts)

### Filler Transitions

Transitions that signal "I need to connect these paragraphs":

- "That being said..."
- "With that in mind..."
- "Having said that..."
- "On a related note..."
- "Moving forward..."

## Structural Patterns

### The Rule of Three

AI gravitates toward three-item lists, three-part structures, and triple adjectives:

- "X, Y, and Z" (always exactly three)
- Three bullet points when two or four would be more natural
- "It's fast, reliable, and scalable" - triple stacking

**Why it's a tell**: Humans vary list length naturally. Sometimes it's two things, sometimes five. Always-three is a pattern signal.

### Formal Stacking Connectors

Using formal connectors to link paragraphs in sequence:

- "Moreover..."
- "Furthermore..."
- "Additionally..."
- "In addition..."
- "Consequently..."
- "Subsequently..."

**Why it's a tell**: These connectors are fine individually, but AI uses them as default paragraph openers, creating a repetitive cadence that human writers avoid.

### Negative Parallelism

- "Not only X, but also Y"
- "It's not just about X, it's about Y"
- "The question isn't X, it's Y"
- "It's less about X and more about Y"

**Why it's a tell**: Humans use these occasionally. AI uses them in nearly every persuasive passage.

### The Reframe Pivot

Setting up a common understanding, then pivoting to a different perspective:

- "Many people think X. But the reality is Y."
- "The conventional wisdom says X. However..."
- "At first glance, X. But look closer and..."

**Why it's a tell**: Effective rhetorical device that AI overuses to the point of cliche.

### Symmetrical Closings

Mirroring the opening or wrapping with an inspirational bow:

- "At the end of the day..."
- "The bottom line is..."
- "Ultimately..."
- "In conclusion..."
- Restating the thesis in a "full circle" way

### Paragraph Template

AI paragraphs often follow this template:
1. Topic sentence (claim)
2. Elaboration (explain claim)
3. Example or evidence
4. Transition to next point

**Why it's a tell**: This is fine for formal essays but makes casual writing sound robotic.

## Vocabulary Tells

### Overused Verbs

- "Leverage" (instead of use)
- "Utilize" (instead of use)
- "Navigate" (metaphorical, "navigate challenges")
- "Foster" (instead of encourage/build)
- "Streamline" (instead of simplify)
- "Empower" (instead of help/enable)
- "Spearhead" (instead of lead)
- "Facilitate" (instead of help with)
- "Optimize" (overused outside engineering)

### Overused Adjectives/Adverbs

- "Seamless" / "Seamlessly"
- "Holistic"
- "Nuanced" (especially "nuanced understanding")
- "Comprehensive"
- "Actionable"
- "Meaningful" (as in "meaningful impact")
- "Thoughtful" (as in "thoughtful approach")
- "Intentional"

### Overused Nouns

- "Landscape" (as in "competitive landscape")
- "Framework"
- "Journey" (as in "learning journey", "user journey" in non-UX contexts)
- "Ecosystem"
- "Paradigm"
- "Synergy"
- "Stakeholders"
- "Takeaways"

## Tone Tells

### Excessive Positivity

AI defaults to optimistic, encouraging tone. Signs:
- Every challenge has a silver lining
- Problems are always "opportunities"
- Endings are always hopeful
- Criticism is always "constructive"

### False Certainty

Stating opinions as facts, or hedging everything uniformly:
- "Research shows..." (without citation)
- "Experts agree..."
- "Studies have demonstrated..."

### Performative Empathy

- "I understand how frustrating this must be"
- "This can feel overwhelming"
- "You're not alone in feeling this way"

## Anti-Voice Archetypes

These are common voice "characters" that people often want to distance themselves from. During the Anti-Voice Definition phase of the questionnaire, the user selects which archetypes their voice is explicitly NOT. All associated phrases and patterns from the selected archetypes get added to the rejection list.

Each archetype represents a cluster of habits that, taken together, produce a recognizable and often unwanted voice. A writer might use one or two phrases from an archetype without sounding like it -- the archetype becomes a problem when the patterns stack up.

---

### The Corporate Blogger

Writes like a company blog that has been through three rounds of stakeholder review. Everything is polished to the point of saying nothing. The goal seems to be sounding professional rather than communicating.

**Telltale phrases:**
- "Leverage our core competencies"
- "Drive impact across the organization"
- "At the end of the day"
- "Moving forward"
- "Align on key deliverables"
- "Circle back on this"
- "Double down on what's working"
- "Low-hanging fruit"
- "Best-in-class"
- "Value proposition"
- "Thought leadership"
- "Synergize efforts"

**Structural patterns:**
- Every post has "Key Takeaways" in a bulleted list at the top or bottom
- Excessive use of bullet points and sub-headers -- even for short pieces
- Paragraphs rarely exceed three sentences
- Always includes a call to action at the end, whether or not one is needed
- Content is organized around "pillars" or "frameworks"

**What makes this voice recognizable:** The relentless focus on sounding strategic. Nothing is ever just done; it's "executed." Nothing is ever useful; it delivers "value." The writing treats the reader as a stakeholder to be managed rather than a person to be talked to.

---

### The LinkedIn Thought Leader

Performs insight for an audience. Every post is a lesson, every experience is a parable, every observation needs to land like a TED talk closer. The writing is less about sharing ideas and more about being seen sharing ideas.

**Telltale phrases:**
- "I'm thrilled to announce"
- "Here's what I learned"
- "Here's why that matters"
- "Let that sink in"
- "Read that again"
- "Most people won't tell you this, but..."
- "I failed. And it was the best thing that ever happened to me."
- "Agree?"
- "Stop doing X. Start doing Y."
- "The secret? It's not what you think."
- "If you're not doing X, you're falling behind"
- "Grateful for this journey"

**Structural patterns:**
- Numbered list posts ("7 things I wish I knew about...")
- One-sentence paragraphs stacked for dramatic effect
- Performative humility before a humble-brag
- Stories that conveniently prove the author's expertise
- Ending with a question to prompt engagement
- Line breaks after every sentence for visual "punch"

**What makes this voice recognizable:** The cadence of revelation. Everything is framed as hard-won wisdom being generously shared. The writer positions themselves as slightly ahead of the reader on the same path, offering a hand back. The tone swings between confessional vulnerability and authoritative confidence.

---

### The Textbook

Writes as if every piece of text is an academic reference work. Precision is valued over readability. The voice has no personality because personality would be imprecise.

**Telltale phrases:**
- "It should be noted that"
- "The reader will observe"
- "As previously mentioned"
- "It is important to recognize"
- "One must consider"
- "This phenomenon is characterized by"
- "The aforementioned"
- "In the context of"
- "It is generally accepted that"
- "With respect to"
- "Per the above"
- "Hereafter referred to as"

**Structural patterns:**
- Relentless passive voice ("It was determined that..." instead of "We found...")
- No contractions, ever
- Formal topic sentences for every paragraph
- Heavy use of nominalizations ("the utilization of" instead of "using")
- Excessive qualifying ("in most cases", "generally speaking", "to a certain extent")
- Information organized taxonomically rather than narratively

**What makes this voice recognizable:** The complete absence of the writer as a person. The text reads as though it was produced by an institution rather than an individual. Every sentence is defensible but none are memorable.

---

### The Hype Machine

Everything is incredible, revolutionary, and about to change the world. This voice cannot describe anything without making it sound like a product launch. Enthusiasm has replaced substance.

**Telltale phrases:**
- "Game-changing"
- "Revolutionary"
- "Groundbreaking"
- "Next-level"
- "Disrupting the industry"
- "Unprecedented"
- "Absolutely incredible"
- "Mind-blowing"
- "The future is here"
- "This changes everything"
- "You won't believe"
- "Jaw-dropping"

**Structural patterns:**
- Superlatives everywhere -- nothing is good, only "the best"
- Breathless short sentences for manufactured urgency
- Heavy use of exclamation marks
- Comparisons always favor the subject ("unlike anything you've seen before")
- Problems are always "solved" completely, never partially improved
- Every feature is "powerful" or "incredible"

**What makes this voice recognizable:** The inability to calibrate enthusiasm. A minor UI improvement gets the same rhetorical treatment as a cure for cancer. The reader learns to trust nothing because everything is equally amazing.

---

### The AI Default

The voice that emerges when a language model is given no strong stylistic direction. It is a blend of every pattern in this catalog, producing text that is fluent, correct, and completely anonymous. This is the baseline that lexisim exists to help writers escape.

**Telltale phrases:**
- All hedging and qualifying phrases from the Common AI Phrases section
- All generic openers ("In today's fast-paced world...")
- All filler transitions ("That being said...")
- All empty emphasis words ("Truly", "Remarkably", "Transformative")
- All forced engagement phrases ("Let's dive in...", "Let's unpack this...")
- All setup labels ("The key insight:", "Here's what matters:")
- All overused vocabulary ("leverage", "navigate", "seamless", "holistic", "landscape", "framework")

**Structural patterns:**
- Rule of three applied to almost every list
- Formal stacking connectors between paragraphs
- The claim-elaborate-example-transition paragraph template
- Symmetrical openings and closings
- Negative parallelism in persuasive passages
- Reframe pivots used as a default rhetorical move
- Excessive positivity and false certainty in tone
- Performative empathy when addressing the reader

**What makes this voice recognizable:** Nothing stands out and nothing offends. The writing is competent, polished, and interchangeable with any other AI-generated text. It reads like a well-trained average of the internet -- technically proficient but with no point of view, no rough edges, and no signature.

---

### The Hustle Bro

Writes like a motivational poster that discovered caffeine. Every topic is filtered through a lens of productivity, optimization, and winning. Rest is failure. Nuance is weakness. The reader should feel both inspired and slightly inadequate.

**Telltale phrases:**
- "Rise and grind"
- "10x your output"
- "No excuses"
- "While you were sleeping, I was..."
- "Outwork everyone in the room"
- "The hustle never stops"
- "Winners don't make excuses"
- "Execute, execute, execute"
- "Monetize your passion"
- "Build your empire"
- "Level up"
- "Stay hungry"

**Structural patterns:**
- Short, punchy paragraphs -- often one sentence
- Imperative mood: commands rather than suggestions
- Binary framing: you're either growing or dying
- Name-dropping successful entrepreneurs as proof of concept
- Mornings described in military detail (4 AM wake-up, cold shower, journaling)
- Lists of "non-negotiable" habits or rules

**What makes this voice recognizable:** The relentless forward motion. There is no room for ambiguity, rest, or the possibility that hard work alone is insufficient. Complex problems have simple solutions, and those solutions always involve more effort. The voice sounds like it's yelling at you from the other side of a gym.

---

### The Academic

Writes to be cited, not read. Every claim requires a caveat. Every statement is hedged into near-meaninglessness. The voice prioritizes showing the writer has read everything over communicating anything clearly.

**Telltale phrases:**
- "The literature suggests"
- "A growing body of evidence indicates"
- "This is not without its limitations"
- "Further research is needed"
- "It is beyond the scope of this"
- "Scholars have long debated"
- "This problematizes the notion that"
- "Through the lens of"
- "Discourse"
- "Praxis"
- "Hegemonic"
- "Intersectionality of these factors"

**Structural patterns:**
- Extremely long sentences with multiple embedded clauses
- Parenthetical citations mid-sentence that break reading flow
- Extensive use of passive voice to avoid first person
- Every paragraph builds on the previous one in a chain of qualifications
- Conclusions that restate the problem rather than resolving it
- Heavy use of jargon when plain language would serve

**What makes this voice recognizable:** The defensive posture. Every sentence is written as though a peer reviewer is looking over the writer's shoulder, ready to pounce on any claim that isn't sufficiently qualified. The result is prose that is technically unassailable but exhausting to read.

---

### The Breathless Storyteller

Writes everything as narrative, even when the content doesn't warrant it. Every blog post begins with a scene. Every product description is a journey. The writer believes that "show, don't tell" means never, ever telling.

**Telltale phrases:**
- "It was a Tuesday afternoon when..."
- "Picture this:"
- "Imagine you're..."
- "Little did they know"
- "And then something unexpected happened"
- "Fast forward to today"
- "That's when everything changed"
- "The rest, as they say, is history"
- "But here's where it gets interesting"
- "It all started with a simple question"
- "And I'll never forget what happened next"
- "Spoiler: it worked"

**Structural patterns:**
- Opens with an anecdote regardless of whether the content needs one
- Chronological structure even for non-narrative content
- Manufactured tension and resolution arcs in informational pieces
- Dialogue reconstructed from memory (or invented) to add "color"
- Frequent paragraph breaks for pacing and suspense
- Cliffhanger transitions between sections

**What makes this voice recognizable:** The refusal to just state things plainly. A guide to setting up a database starts with "It was 3 AM and the server was on fire." Information that could be a clear paragraph becomes a three-act drama. The storytelling is a wrapper that the reader has to unwrap to find the actual content.

## Using This Reference

### During Phase 6 (Anti-Voice Definition)

1. Present the Anti-Voice Archetypes to the user
2. The user selects which archetypes their voice is explicitly NOT
3. All phrases and patterns from selected archetypes get added to the rejection list
4. Present additional selections from the Common AI Phrases and other categories
5. When the user rejects a phrase, expand to the full category
6. Ask for any additional patterns they've noticed

### When Building the Rejection List

For each rejected archetype or category, include:
- The specific rejected phrases
- The expanded category phrases
- Structural patterns to avoid
- A brief note on what to use instead (from the user's own writing samples)

### In the Writer Skill

The generated writer skill should include a pre-delivery check:
1. Scan output for any phrase on the rejection list
2. If found, rewrite the sentence using the user's natural patterns
3. Check for structural patterns (rule of three, formal stacking, archetype-specific structures) if those were rejected
4. Verify tone doesn't match any rejected archetype's signature characteristics
5. Confirm the output wouldn't be mistaken for any of the user's selected anti-voice archetypes
